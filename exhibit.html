Enlightenment and Empire

Zen Buddhism, Nationalist Violence, and the Problem of Doctrinal Susceptibility

The fusion of Zen and Japanese militarism was enabled not only by institutional pressure but by a structural susceptibility within Zen doctrine itself. The same antinomian logic that rendered killing spiritually intelligible during the war foreclosed moral reckoning afterward. Nakajima Genjō’s postwar denial of the Nanjing Massacre was not a rupture from his wartime framework but its continuation.

Case Study: Rinzai Zen Master Nakajima Genjō (1915–2000), Shōinji Temple, Hara, Shizuoka Prefecture

Node I · Doctrinal Foundation

"Transcending Good and Evil"

Rinzai Zen is not a monolith. Its antinomian strand has been contested across centuries. By the 1930s, however, that strand had been elevated into the dominant self-presentation of the Rinzai establishment, through institutional changes that began with the Meiji Restoration. Robert Sharf, in “The Zen of Japanese Nationalism” (1993), argues that “Zen” as a distinct spiritual identity was largely a Meiji-era construction. Nationalism was not imposed on Zen from outside; it was built into modern Zen’s institutional form. This node examines that antinomian claim: that the enlightened transcend moral duality, and how that claim was used to authorize wartime killing.

Primary Source I · An 8th-Century Warning About Antinomianism

Primary Source · Liangsu (753–793 CE), Chinese Writer and Buddhist Scholar

“Nowadays, few men have true faith. Those who travel the path of Chan go so far as to teach the people that there is neither Buddha nor Dharma, and that neither good nor evil has any significance. When they preach these doctrines to the average man, or men below average, they are believed by all those who live their lives of worldly desires. Such ideas are accepted as great truths which sound so pleasing to the ear. And the people are attracted to them just as moths in the night are drawn to their burning death by the candle light.”

Liangsu (753–793 CE), as cited in Brian Victoria, Zen War Stories (RoutledgeCurzon, 2003), p. 34. Liangsu was a student of Tiantai Buddhism who criticized the antinomian tendencies of Chinese Chan as early as the 8th century.
Analytical Commentary

Liangsu was identifying a problem that would recur. The antinomian claim, that “neither good nor evil has any significance,” does not originate in Japanese militarism. It has a long history within Chan and Zen and has been contested within the tradition for just as long. The 20th-century Japanese case shows what happens when one side of that debate achieves institutional dominance and encounters a state demanding ideological alignment.

Victoria cites Liangsu directly: “It is only when we become aware of Zen’s deep-seated antinomianism … that Genjō’s attitude … becomes comprehensible.” (Victoria, Zen War Stories, p. 34) The candle that draws moths to their death is a doctrine that sounds like freedom from all constraint. This is not an argument that Zen produced violence. It is an argument that its antinomian strand created resources that could be mobilized, under specific conditions, to make violence spiritually intelligible.

Note on sources: Victoria is both the primary translator of Genjō’s autobiography and the scholar who frames the case analytically. No other complete English translation of Yasoji o koete exists, so the exhibit depends on his renderings. This matters more than it might seem. Victoria is not a neutral translator, he is the scholar who built the entire critical framework this exhibit adopts. His argument, developed across two books, is that Zen institutions bear real moral responsibility for the Pacific War. That shapes which passages he chose to translate, how he introduces them, and what he dwells on. The passages in this exhibit are, more precisely, Victoria’s selections from a text he found damning. A reader with access to the full Japanese autobiography might draw the picture differently. The exhibit’s argument is persuasive, but it rests on one voice, and that voice is also a prosecutor. Robert Sharf (“The Zen of Japanese Nationalism,” 1993) provides partial independent corroboration: he argues that the Zen-nationalism alignment was constitutive of modern Zen’s identity rather than a wartime aberration. A note on language: all sources in this exhibit are in English. For a project on Japanese Buddhist institutional history, that is a real constraint. The English-language tradition of Zen studies has its own politics, and those politics are, ironically, part of what the exhibit analyzes. D.T. Suzuki’s influence on how Western audiences understood Zen is one of the exhibit’s own conditions of production. Japanese scholarship on wartime Buddhism, including Ichikawa Hakugen’s postwar self-critique and subsequent responses to Victoria from within Japanese Buddhist studies, lies outside this exhibit’s reach.

Primary Source II · Yamamoto Gempō’s 1934 Proclamation

Primary Source · Yamamoto Gempō (1866–1961), Rinzai Zen Master, 1934

“The Buddha, being absolute, has stated that when there are those who destroy social harmony and injure the polity of the state, then killing them is not a crime.”

Yamamoto Gempō, 1934, as cited in Brian Victoria, Zen War Stories (RoutledgeCurzon, 2003), p. 33. Gempō was abbot of Shōinji and Ryūtakuji temples and one of the most highly respected Rinzai masters of the modern era. He was Nakajima Genjō’s master.
Analytical Commentary

Gempō’s 1934 proclamation states the logic plainly. “The Buddha, being absolute” occupies the position of ultimate reality, beyond ordinary moral distinctions. From that position, the category of “crime” does not apply to killing the state’s enemies. This is a doctrinal claim: those who act from the perspective of absolute Buddha-nature are not subject to the moral prohibition against killing, which belongs to the domain of conventional, unawakened judgment.

In Genjō’s case, the claim carries more than institutional authority. In Rinzai Zen, a master’s word carries soteriological authority: the authority of someone who has actually realized the non-dual perspective being described. When Gempō told Genjō that military service was Dharma practice, he was not endorsing a policy. He was certifying, as a recognized enlightened master, that the antinomian reading was correct. That authority bound Genjō in a way that external pressure alone could not. Victoria notes that Genjō had trained under Gempō for nine years before voluntarily enlisting. He enlisted in 1936, the year after his initial kenshō. He did so through this teaching, not despite it. (Victoria, Zen War Stories, pp. 21–22)

A counter-argument deserves real engagement. Ichikawa Hakugen and other postwar critics within Zen argued that institutional survival under state coercion was the primary driver of Buddhist militarism, and that doctrinal susceptibility matters less than political pressure. Ichikawa’s own postwar self-critique shows that dissent was possible within the same tradition. The doctrine alone does not determine the outcome. But Genjō’s case is hard for the coercion explanation: enlistment was voluntary, framed in doctrinal terms, experienced as spiritual deepening. The institutional and doctrinal explanations are not mutually exclusive. Ichikawa shows the doctrine could go otherwise; Genjō shows that in this instance it did not.

Theoretical Analysis
Juergensmeyer · Cosmic War (Corrective Application)

Mark Juergensmeyer argues in Terror in the Mind of God that religious violence characteristically operates through a “cosmic war” framework: earthly violence reframed as a transcendent battle between good and evil. The Zen case does not fit this structure. Gempō’s proclamation does not pit sacred Japan against a demonized enemy in a drama of cosmic conflict. Instead, it operates through the antinomian move of dissolving the good/evil distinction altogether. The license for violence comes not from elevating one side of a moral binary but from transcending the binary itself. This is a variant Juergensmeyer’s framework does not fully account for. It should be noted that the application here is corrective rather than confirmatory: using a framework to mark where it stops fitting is a different operation than using it to explain. The Zen case suggests that antinomian dissolution of moral categories may be analytically distinct from cosmic war, and that comparative work on religious violence needs to accommodate both.

Ketelaar · Meiji Institutional Realignment

James Ketelaar’s account of the Meiji Restoration shows that Buddhist institutions’ embrace of imperial ideology was a survival strategy: Buddhism had been violently suppressed as “foreign” and anti-nationalist, and Buddhist leaders learned to demonstrate their nationalist credentials as a condition of survival. The doctrinal susceptibility Gempō’s proclamation displays did not emerge in a vacuum; it emerged from a century of institutional pressure in which Buddhist traditions were rewarded for aligning with imperial ambitions. This institutional history is what made Gempō’s proclamation possible.

Sharf / Victoria · The Modern Construction of “Zen”

Victoria’s account in Zen at War, 2nd ed. (2006), shows that Gempō’s antinomian logic was not isolated but spread across Rinzai institutions as “imperial-state Zen” (kōkoku Zen). Lt. Col. Sugimoto Gorō (1900–1937): “The wars of the empire are sacred wars. They are holy wars. They are the [Buddhist] practice of great compassion. Therefore the imperial military must consist of holy officers and holy soldiers.” In a separate passage on war and self-cultivation: “War is moral training for not only the individual but for the entire world. It consists of the extinction of self-seeking and the destruction of self-preservation. It is only those without self-attachment who are able to revere the emperor absolutely.” Yamazaki Ekijū (1882–1961), chief abbot of the Buttsūji branch of the Rinzai Zen sect, endorsed Sugimoto’s writings in a 104-page eulogy appended to the published volume. Sharf’s account adds something further. The construction of “Zen” as Japan’s distinctive spiritual contribution to world civilization, associated especially with D.T. Suzuki’s influential writings in the 1920s and 1930s, shaped how practitioners understood the tradition they inhabited. By the time Genjō enlisted, the antinomian reading had been elevated into what “real Zen” meant, crowding out other readings that had coexisted within the tradition for centuries.

Node II · Enlistment & Practice

"Kōans on a Battleship"

Nakajima Genjō voluntarily enlisted in the Imperial Japanese Navy in 1936, with his master Gempō’s blessing, and continued his kōan practice by correspondence from the battleship Ise. His own words show enlistment experienced not as a departure from Zen practice but as its deepening. The battlefield becomes the “great kōan.”

Primary Source III · Gempō’s Farewell at the Temple Gate

Primary Source · Nakajima Genjō, Autobiography Yasoji o koete (Beyond Eighty Years), 1999, via Victoria

“I enlisted in the Imperial Japanese Navy in 1936. On the morning I was to leave, Master [Yamamoto] Gempō accompanied me as far as the entrance to the temple grounds. He pointed to a nearby small shed housing a water wheel and said: ‘Look at that water wheel, as long as there is water, the wheel keeps turning. The wheel of the Dharma is the same. As long as the self-sacrificing mind of a bodhisattva is present, the Dharma is realized. You must exert yourself to the utmost to ensure that the water of the bodhisattva mind never runs out.’”

Nakajima Genjō, Yasoji o koete (Beyond Eighty Years) (1999), as translated and cited in Brian Victoria, Zen War Stories (RoutledgeCurzon, 2003), p. 22. Victoria notes that Gempō required Genjō to depart in the garb of an itinerant monk (robes, conical wicker hat, and straw sandals) rather than the uniform of the youth corps.
Analytical Commentary

Gempō’s farewell fuses military service and Buddhist practice through the figure of the bodhisattva. The water wheel metaphor is specific: just as the wheel turns by water, the Dharma is realized through self-sacrifice. Military deployment and monastic training are made equivalent: both are conditions under which the bodhisattva mind either sustains or fails. Departure is not a break from practice but its extension into a different terrain.

The bodhisattva framing converts the nation’s demand for self-sacrifice into an occasion for practice. The soldier who “exerts himself to the utmost” is not just serving the emperor; he is keeping the water of the Dharma flowing. The doctrinal and the political are not in tension. They speak the same language.

The self-sacrificing bodhisattva mind is, in this formulation, the operative concept. In Mahāyāna Buddhist tradition, the bodhisattva vow involves delaying one’s own liberation in order to serve sentient beings. Gempō’s invocation of this concept in the context of military enlistment performs the same antinomian move as his 1934 proclamation: it appropriates a classical Buddhist framework and redirects it toward nationalist service, so that Genjō’s participation in imperial war becomes legible as a form of compassionate, selfless action.

Primary Source IV · Kōan Practice by Correspondence from the Battleship

Primary Source · Nakajima Genjō, Autobiography (via Victoria)

“In the summer of 1937 I was granted a short leave and returned to visit Master Gempō at Shōinji. It was clear that the master was not the least bit worried that I might die in battle. ‘Even if a bullet comes your way,’ he said, ‘it will swerve around you.’ I replied, ‘But bullets don’t swerve!’ ‘Don’t tell me that,’ he remonstrated, ‘you came back this time, didn’t you?’ ‘Yes, that’s true …’ I said, and we both had a good laugh. While I was at Shōinji that summer I successfully answered the Master’s final queries concerning the kōan known as ‘Zhaozhou’s Mu’… I had grappled with this kōan for some five years, even in the midst of my life in the navy.”

Nakajima Genjō, Yasoji o koete (1999), via Victoria, Zen War Stories (2003), p. 22. The kōan “Zhaozhou’s Mu” is the introductory kōan of the Rinzai curriculum, the standard first assignment for students seeking kenshō. Gempō subsequently assigned Genjō “the most difficult kōan of all,” the sound of one hand, and agreed to receive Genjō’s responses by letter rather than in person.
Plate 3 – The Japanese Imperial Navy battleship Ise, on which Genjō initially served. Genjō continued his kōan practice by correspondence with Master Gempō from this ship. Courtesy Nakajima Genjō.
Analytical Commentary

In his own account, naval service and Zen training ran together, not in sequence. Genjō grappled with “Zhaozhou’s Mu,” the first kōan in the Rinzai curriculum, “for some five years, even in the midst of my life in the navy.” Gempō agreed to receive Genjō’s kōan responses by letter. Victoria notes this “did not represent any lessening of the master’s severity but was a reflection of his deep affection for the Buddha Dharma.” The ritual structure of the master-disciple relationship was maintained across the distance of the Pacific theater: the formal exchange, the assignment of kōans, the recognition of insight. Gempō’s equanimity about Genjō’s possible death in battle reflects the Rinzai teaching that life and death are “one.” Confronting this, whether on a battlefield or in a meditation hall, is the work of enlightenment.

Gempō agreed to receive Genjō’s kōan responses by letter rather than face to face. Victoria notes this “did not represent any lessening of the master’s severity but was a reflection of his deep affection for the Buddha Dharma.” Gempō’s laughter at the bullet exchange and his equanimity about Genjō’s possible death in battle are not signs of indifference. They follow from the Rinzai teaching that life and death are “one,” and that confronting this fact, whether on a battlefield or in a meditation hall, is the work of enlightenment.

Theoretical Analysis
Anderson · Imagined Communities & Buddhist No-Self

Anderson argues in Imagined Communities that nationalism achieves its power by making self-sacrifice feel meaningful. The soldier dies for a community he can never fully know. Zen’s doctrine of no-self adds something Anderson’s analysis does not reach: if the self is already, doctrinally, empty, then dissolving it into the national community is not a sacrifice. It is the completion of what practice was already doing. The nation provides the collective form; Zen doctrine provides the basis on which merging into it can feel like liberation rather than loss.

The transmission system matters here. Gempō’s blessing did not reach Genjō as an institutional directive. It reached him as a pronouncement from someone whose enlightenment qualified him to speak from the non-dual perspective. Dissolving the self into national service was not just culturally available. The chain of transmission had certified it.

Durkheim · Collective Effervescence

Durkheim’s collective effervescence describes the heightened feeling generated by shared ritual practice. But the concept needs to do specific work here, or it adds nothing that Victoria and Sharf don’t already cover. Victoria explains what Gempō taught. Sharf explains how “Zen” was institutionally constructed. What neither quite reaches is why Genjō felt all this as genuine liberation, as his own discovery, rather than as pressure from above. That’s where Durkheim is useful. The kōan correspondence wasn’t just pastoral care: it was the ongoing ritual mechanism that kept generating the feeling of sacred obligation. The practice hadn’t stopped at the dock; it had followed Genjō onto the ship. Regular tests, acknowledgment of his progress, the sense of still being held within a transmission lineage, all of that continued. And it was that felt connection, not just the doctrinal argument, that made military service feel like practice rather than departure from it. (Victoria, Zen War Stories, pp. 22–23)

Node III · Self-Insulation

"The Jinshansi Encounter"

Entering the temple of Jinshansi in Zhenjiang during the China campaign, Genjō confronted the Chinese abbot, and was confronted in return. His account of this encounter reveals what may be the antinomian logic’s most distinctive feature: its capacity to convert a moment of moral challenge into a confirmation of spiritual identity, leaving behavior unchanged.

Menon: Secularism as Misdirection Asad: Formations of the Secular Victoria: Author’s Remarks on Pacifism

Primary Source V · The Jinshansi Dialogue

Primary Source · Nakajima Genjō, Autobiography (via Victoria)

“On entering the temple grounds I came across some five hundred novice monks practicing meditation in the meditation hall. As I was still young and immature, I blurted out to the abbot, ‘What do you think you’re doing! In Japan everyone is consumed by the war with China, and this is all you can do?’ [The abbot replied,] ‘And just who are you to talk! I hear that you are a priest. War is for soldiers. A priest’s work is to read the sūtras and meditate!’

The abbot didn’t say any more than this, but I felt as if I had been hit on the head with a sledgehammer. As a result I immediately became a pacifist.”

Nakajima Genjō, Yasoji o koete (1999), via Victoria, Zen War Stories (2003), p. 26. Jinshansi (Gold Mountain Temple) is located in Zhenjiang, Jiangsu province. It is the temple where Kūkai, the 9th-century founder of the esoteric Shingon sect in Japan, had studied on his way to Chang’an.
Analytical Commentary

The Jinshansi encounter is, as Victoria writes, “one of the most memorable parts of his memoirs, for it clearly reveals a head-on clash of values. On the one side stands Buddhism’s universal commitment to non-killing. On the other side is the Japanese military’s willingness to engage in mass killing as an instrument of national policy. Genjō was a Buddhist priest, yet he was also a member of the Japanese military. What was he to do?”

The abbot’s rebuke (I hear that you are a priest. War is for soldiers.) lands on Genjō with the force of a “sledgehammer.” His response is immediate: “As a result I immediately became a pacifist.” The language mirrors the sudden enlightenment (satori) that Rinzai training aims at. The experience is real; Genjō is not lying about how he felt.

And yet nothing changed. Victoria notes: “As promising as his dramatic change of heart first appears, nowhere does Genjō demonstrate that it had the slightest effect on his subsequent conduct, i.e. on his willingness to fight and kill in the name of the emperor.” By treating inner experience as the only thing that counts, the antinomian logic allows genuine inner transformation (feeling, conviction, even “pacifism”) to coexist with continued external violence. Interior conviction and exterior conduct are placed in separate registers.

Primary Source VI · Great Enlightenment Adrift at Sea

Primary Source · Nakajima Genjō, Autobiography (via Victoria)

“It was also in the spring of [1943] that my ship was hit by a torpedo off the coast of Hainan island. As I drifted in the South China Sea, I groaned caught in the realm of desire, hovering between life and death. Kōans and reciting the name of Amitābha Buddha were meaningless. There was nothing else to do but totally devote myself to Zen practice within the context of the ocean itself. It would be a shame to die here I thought, for I wanted to return to being a Zen priest. Therefore I single-mindedly devoted myself to making every possible effort to survive, abandoning all thought of life and death. It was just at that moment that I freed myself from life and death.

This freedom from life and death was in reality the realization of great enlightenment (daigo). I placed my hands together in my mind and bowed down to venerate the Buddha, the Zen Patriarchs, their Dharma descendants, and especially Master Gempō.”

Nakajima Genjō, Yasoji o koete (1999), via Victoria, Zen War Stories (2003), p. 27. Victoria notes that the torpedo sank Genjō’s second ship, the military fuel tanker Ryōhei, killing some thirteen of his shipmates.
Analytical Commentary

This passage traces the logic of antinomian Zen to its end point. When ordinary religious support fails (kōans and nembutsu become “meaningless”), Genjō turns to the immediate situation: the ocean itself, the real possibility of death. He abandons “all thought of life and death” and claims to realize freedom from that duality. The battlefield has delivered exactly what Gempō promised.

Victoria puts it directly: “In Genjō’s case it is even possible to argue that his experience of ‘great enlightenment’ was actually hastened by his military service, specifically the fortuitous American torpedo that sank his second ship.” The war did not interrupt his practice. It accelerated it.

This is the self-insulating structure completed. The framework does not merely accommodate violence; it retrospectively incorporates the violence into the narrative of the practitioner’s spiritual progress. The thirteen men who died in the same attack that set Genjō adrift do not appear in this passage. What the framework renders visible is Genjō’s interior realization; what it renders invisible is its human cost.

Theoretical Analysis
Victoria · The Limits of Genjō’s Pacifism

During a second visit to Shōinji in January 2000, Victoria directly asked Genjō why he had not attempted to distance himself from Japan’s war effort following his conversion to pacifism at Jinshansi. “His reply was short and to the point: ‘I would have been court-martialled and shot had I done so.’” Victoria acknowledges the honesty of this answer and does not dismiss it. But he notes that it coexists with Genjō’s persistent self-presentation as “the very embodiment of the Buddha’s enlightenment.” The question, Victoria observes, is whether “the killing of countless human beings in order to save one’s own life [is] an authentic expression of the Buddha Dharma, of the Buddha’s enlightenment.”

Menon · Secularism as Misdirection

Menon argues in Secularism as Misdirection that secularism makes certain political operations invisible by marking them as beyond politics. The parallel here is structural: invoking spiritual transcendence moves Genjō’s wartime conduct into a register that ordinary moral criticism cannot easily reach. The Jinshansi “conversion” is a case in point. A genuine interior experience seals the self from accountability. Not by denying the experience, but by treating interior transformation as sufficient. Menon’s argument is about secularism as a tool of governance, how the secular state makes certain things invisible by classifying them as “religious” and therefore outside political scrutiny. That’s not exactly what happens with Genjō. But the move is the same shape: a claim to transcendence puts a domain beyond ordinary criticism. For Genjō, the transcendence claim is spiritual. He has “become a pacifist” in his inner life, and that inner fact is what counts. What happens outside, the bodies at Nanjing, the survivors’ accounts, doesn’t enter the frame, not because he suppresses it, but because his framework places moral accountability somewhere that external evidence simply can’t reach. Victoria’s account shows that this happened; Menon helps explain why it’s so hard to argue with from the outside. It isn’t that Genjō is lying. The structure makes him genuinely immune to correction. (Victoria, Zen War Stories, pp. 26–27)

Node IV · Postwar Denial

"Postwar Silence: Denying Nanjing"

In his 1999 autobiography, written more than fifty years after the war, Nakajima Genjō denied that the Nanjing Massacre occurred. Read against the prior three nodes, this denial is not merely political calculation. It is the same logic at work: foreclosing moral reckoning by the same means that made violence spiritually intelligible in the first place.

Anderson: National Memory & Forgetting Menon: Selective Visibility Victoria: Author’s Remarks on Nanjing

Primary Source VII · Genjō’s Denial of the Nanjing Massacre

Primary Source · Nakajima Genjō, Autobiography (via Victoria)

“Not long after this came the capture of Nanjing (Nanking). Actually we were able to capture it without much of a fight at all. I have heard people claim that a great massacre took place at Nanjing, but I am firmly convinced there was no such thing. It was wartime, however, so there may have been a little trouble with the women. In any event, after things start to settle down, it is pretty difficult to kill anyone.”

Nakajima Genjō, Yasoji o koete (1999), via Victoria, Zen War Stories (2003), p. 26. These words were written in 1999, more than fifty years after the events at Nanjing. Victoria notes that Genjō served as a shipboard sailor during the Nanjing campaign.
Analytical Commentary

Three sentences do most of the work. The first asserts ease of capture to minimize what followed. The second acknowledges having “heard people claim” a massacre occurred and rejects it without evidence. The third is the most troubling: “there may have been a little trouble with the women.” It concedes the existence of mass sexual violence while reducing it to an inconvenience, acknowledging and erasing at the same time.

The denial sits against an established evidentiary record. The Nanjing Massacre is documented by the International Military Tribunal for the Far East (IMTFE, Chapter VIII, p. 1009, 1948); by John Rabe, a German businessman who remained in Nanjing throughout the occupation and kept a daily record (The Good Man of Nanking, trans. John E. Woods, Knopf, 1998); and by Japanese military documentation entered as evidence at trial. Genjō’s “firm conviction” is not a considered engagement with competing evidence. It is an assertion made from outside the ordinary standards of historical evidence.

The same logic at work in the Jinshansi encounter operates here. Moral significance locates entirely in the interior register. “I am firmly convinced” reads not as an empirical claim but as an expression of enlightened certainty. That certainty, by definition, cannot be corrected by what happened to people who were never visible within this framework. (Victoria, Zen War Stories, pp. 26–27)

Primary Source VIII · Victoria’s Analysis: The Tears Were Different

Analytical Source · Brian Victoria, Author’s Remarks, Zen War Stories (2003)

“Upon reading Genjō’s words, I could not help but feel deeply disappointed. This disappointment stemmed from the realization that while I and Genjō had shed tears together, we were crying about profoundly different things. Genjō’s tears were devoted to one thing and one thing only: his fallen comrades… One gets a strong impression that as far as Genjō is concerned what was ‘wrong’ about the ‘Greater East Asia War’ was not the war itself, but that, unlike its earlier wars, Japan had been defeated… The problem was not that Japan had invaded China and occupied numerous Asian countries, let alone attacked the USA, but that his superiors had recklessly stationed troops in areas, especially in the South Seas, that were indefensible once Japan lost air superiority.”

Brian Victoria, Zen War Stories (RoutledgeCurzon, 2003), pp. 28–29. Victoria had wept together with Genjō during his 1999 visit, before reading the autobiography. The title of the chapter, “The Zen Master Wept,” refers to this scene.
Analytical Commentary

Victoria and Genjō wept together, but over different things. Genjō’s grief is genuine; Victoria does not dispute this. His grief attaches to his fallen Japanese comrades, to the men who died from disease and starvation in the South Seas. The victims of Japanese aggression do not appear in the field of grief. Not because Genjō suppresses them, but because, within this framework, they were never present to be suppressed.

But the framework determines what the emotion attaches to. It attaches to his fallen Japanese comrades, to the waste of men who died from disease and starvation in the South Seas because their commanders were incompetent. It does not attach, and by the logic of the framework cannot attach, to the victims of Japanese aggression: those are the events that belong to the external world of war, which the enlightened practitioner’s interior realization has already transcended.

Calling this hypocrisy misses the mechanism. Hypocrisy involves knowing the standard and failing it. The hypocrite at least maintains a relationship to the moral framework being violated. What Genjō’s case shows is something else: a framework in which the victims of Japanese aggression are not merely ignored but not visible at all. As Victoria writes, “it is as if they never existed.” These words come from a man writing in 1999, at eighty-four, after a lifetime of Buddhist practice. The framework had not weakened. In its postwar form, it achieved something the wartime version could not: the quiet, permanent closure of any reckoning with what Japan had done. (Victoria, Zen War Stories, pp. 28–29)

Conclusion: What This Case Reveals

Religion, Fundamentalism, and Nationalism: The Argument’s Reach

A strict reading of this case demands a question the structural argument alone cannot answer: why did the antinomian strand achieve institutional dominance in the 1930s specifically, when it had coexisted with other Zen readings for centuries?

Three conditions converged. First, the Meiji institutional crisis (Ketelaar): Buddhism had been suppressed as “un-Japanese,” and Buddhist institutions spent the following decades demonstrating nationalist credentials as a condition of survival. By the 1930s, ideological alignment was structural, not situational. Second, the modern construction of “Zen” as Japan’s distinctive spiritual contribution to world civilization. Figures like D.T. Suzuki positioned the antinomian reading as the authentic essence of the tradition: martial, disciplined, beyond rationality. Other readings that had coexisted within Zen for centuries were crowded out. Third, the Rinzai transmission system. When a master of Gempō’s standing certified that military service was Dharma practice, that certification carried genuine force. Disciples received it not as institutional pressure but as doctrinal truth.

Structural availability is not determinism. Ichikawa Hakugen drew on the same tradition and arrived at postwar self-critique. The same no-self teaching has grounded, in other contexts, rejections of nationalist categories altogether. What these three conditions made possible was a specific assembly: killing experienced as spiritual liberation, and denial experienced as enlightened certainty. The exhibit traces that assembly. It does not claim it was inevitable.

Victoria notes that Genjō’s autobiography was written not “in the midst of the hysteria of a nation at war, but as recently as 1999, more than fifty years later.” The most consequential operations of this logic may be not in the heat of violence but in the long, quiet decades of its denial.

One question the exhibit can’t answer: is there something specifically dangerous about Zen’s antinomian strand, or would the same assembly have been possible in any tradition given the right institutional conditions, state pressure, a charismatic teacher willing to certify violence as practice, and a transmission system that made that certification feel personally binding? The honest answer is that this exhibit examines one case and can’t say. Other traditions have doctrines that dissolve the individual moral self into something larger. Most of them have not produced this outcome. What the three conditions in this argument, the Meiji crisis, the modern construction of “Zen,” the Rinzai transmission system, try to specify is what turned availability into actuality here. They are meant to be historically specific, not a recipe that any tradition could follow. The argument is not that Zen is uniquely prone to this. It is that, under these conditions, it happened.

About This Exhibit

This digital exhibit was produced as a final project for a course in Religion, Fundamentalism, and Nationalism. It examines how Zen Buddhist institutions became entangled with Japanese militarism in the early twentieth century, focusing on Rinzai Zen Master Nakajima Genjō (1915–2000) as a biographical case study.

The exhibit is organized as four sequential nodes. Each node pairs primary source material drawn verbatim from Brian Victoria’s translations of Genjō’s 1999 autobiography Yasoji o koete, as published in Zen War Stories (2003), Chapter 1, with analytical commentary applying theoretical frameworks from the course. The photographs are courtesy of Nakajima Genjō, reproduced from the same volume.

The viewer moves from doctrine (Node I) to enlistment (Node II) to wartime contradiction (Node III) to postwar denial (Node IV), each node deepening the previous, so that the exhibit’s structure mirrors its argument: the same antinomian doctrinal logic operating at each stage, with compounding consequences.

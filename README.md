# Enlightenment and Empire
### Zen Buddhism, Nationalist Violence, and the Problem of Doctrinal Susceptibility

A digital exhibit produced as a final project for **Religion, Fundamentalism & Nationalism**.

**Author:** Alex Gao · 2026

---

## Overview

This exhibit examines how Zen Buddhist institutions became entangled with Japanese militarism in the early twentieth century, focusing on Rinzai Zen Master Nakajima Genjō (1915–2000) as a biographical case study. The central argument is that the fusion of Zen and Japanese militarism was enabled not only by institutional pressure but by a structural susceptibility within Zen doctrine itself — specifically, the antinomian claim that the enlightened transcend moral duality. The same logic that rendered killing spiritually intelligible during the war foreclosed moral reckoning afterward.

---

## Structure

The exhibit is organized as four sequential nodes, each pairing primary source material with analytical commentary applying theoretical frameworks from the course.

| Node | Title | Focus |
|------|-------|-------|
| I | "Transcending Good and Evil" | The antinomian doctrinal foundation |
| II | "Kōans on a Battleship" | Enlistment as spiritual fulfillment |
| III | "The Jinshansi Encounter" | The self-insulating logic of inner conversion |
| IV | "Postwar Silence: Denying Nanjing" | Foreclosure of moral reckoning |

The viewer moves from doctrine → enlistment → contradiction → denial, each node deepening the previous, so the exhibit's structure mirrors its argument.
